## Why Code Knowledge Is a Development Competency

Development code advisory is not a legal subspecialty — it is a core development competency. The decisions made at the intersection of a project's physical program and the applicable regulatory framework determine what can be built, at what cost, on what timeline, and with what risk exposure.

In Austin and the Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex, the development code environments are meaningfully different — and meaningfully complex. Developers and investors who treat code compliance as a box-checking exercise rather than a strategic design input leave value on the table and expose themselves to risks that experienced advisors routinely avoid.

This article provides a practical overview of the development code environments in Austin and DFW, with specific attention to the provisions that have the most impact on project economics and timeline.

---

## Austin: A Development Code in Transition

Austin's regulatory environment is undergoing the most significant transformation in the city's history. The Land Development Code — Austin's primary instrument of land use regulation — is being revised through a multi-year process that has already produced meaningful changes and will continue to evolve.

### The Legacy LDC: Still Governing Most Projects

Despite years of reform efforts, Austin's legacy Land Development Code (the "old code") continues to govern most of the city. Key provisions that every Austin developer must understand:

**Zoning Districts:** Austin's zoning framework includes residential districts (SF-1 through SF-6, MF-1 through MF-6), commercial districts (LO through CS), and mixed-use districts (MU). The zoning designation determines the permitted uses, maximum height, setbacks, and floor-to-area ratio for a site.

**Compatibility Standards:** Austin's compatibility standards are among the most restrictive in the state. Properties within 540 feet of a single-family or duplex zoned lot are subject to graduated height and setback restrictions — regardless of the subject property's own zoning. A site zoned for 60-foot height may be limited to 40 feet, 30 feet, or less by compatibility standards triggered by adjacent residential zoning. Understanding compatibility exposure before acquiring a site is non-negotiable.

**Capitol View Corridors:** Approximately 30 designated view corridors protect sightlines to the Texas State Capitol from specific public vantage points across Austin. Properties within a Capitol View Corridor face absolute height limits that can be dramatically lower than otherwise applicable zoning would allow. A site in a CVC can be limited to 40 feet despite being zoned for 120 feet.

**Impervious Cover Limits:** Austin's watershed protection program imposes impervious cover limits that vary by watershed classification. In some watersheds, impervious cover limits constrain development density more severely than zoning regulations.

### HOME and Missing Middle: The Infill Revolution

Austin's HOME (Home Options for Middle-income Empowerment) initiative — adopted in phases beginning in 2023 — eliminated single-family-only zoning on all SF-designated lots within Austin city limits. The first phase authorized up to three units on any SF-zoned lot; the second phase removed minimum lot size requirements and allowed up to three units per lot with no required off-street parking.

For infill developers and small-scale investors, HOME represents a fundamental change to the economics of Austin residential land. Sites that were economically limited to a single-family house now support three-unit projects, dramatically improving land utilization and development feasibility.

**The catch:** HOME applies to residential uses. Commercial, mixed-use, and multifamily developments above three units still navigate the traditional zoning and entitlement process.

### Site Plan Process

Any project requiring site plan approval in Austin enters a multi-step review process involving:

1. **Pre-application conference** — required before submission for most projects
2. **Site plan application** — formal submission with drainage, traffic, utility, and environmental review
3. **Staff review** — City departments review the application against applicable code standards
4. **Planning Commission** — contested cases or certain project types require Planning Commission approval
5. **Site plan release** — administrative approval once all reviews are complete

**Timeline reality:** Austin's site plan process is slow. From initial submission to approved site plan, 6–18 months is a reasonable expectation for most commercial projects. Projects with compatibility issues, environmental constraints, or neighbor opposition can take significantly longer.

## DFW: A Fragmented Regulatory Landscape

The Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex presents a fundamentally different regulatory environment than Austin. Rather than a single city code, DFW developers navigate the codes of dozens of independent municipalities — each with its own zoning ordinance, subdivision regulations, and development standards.

### The Municipal Fragmentation Challenge

DFW's jurisdictional fragmentation means that development knowledge is highly location-specific. A developer who knows Dallas's code well may know very little about the codes in Frisco, McKinney, Celina, Prosper, or the other rapidly growing suburban municipalities where much of DFW's development activity is concentrated.

Key DFW municipalities and their general regulatory characteristics:

**City of Dallas:** Dallas has an established urban zoning framework with a mix of traditional districts and newer form-based standards. The city's Planned Development (PD) zoning mechanism is widely used for large or complex projects and allows negotiated development standards through a legislative process.

**Fort Worth:** Fort Worth's development code is generally more developer-friendly than Dallas's. The city has invested in streamlining its development review process and maintaining reasonable timelines. Mixed-use and infill development in the Near Southside and Urban Village areas are governed by specific overlay regulations.

**Suburban Growth Cities:** Frisco, McKinney, Celina, Prosper, Allen, and other Collin County suburbs are among the fastest-growing municipalities in the United States. Their codes have generally been updated to accommodate rapid growth but vary considerably in their specific standards for residential, commercial, and mixed-use development.

### Planned Development Zoning

For any project in DFW that does not fit neatly within conventional base zoning, the Planned Development (PD) designation is the primary tool. A PD is essentially a negotiated ordinance — the municipality and developer agree on a specific set of development standards that apply to the project, documented in an ordinance that is adopted by the city council.

**PD advantages:**
- Flexibility to deviate from standard setbacks, heights, parking, and use restrictions
- Ability to incorporate mixed uses that do not fit a single base zone
- Ability to negotiate phasing, public improvements, and infrastructure commitments

**PD timeline:** Most DFW municipalities require 3–6 months minimum for a PD adoption, including planning commission and city council approval. Complex or politically sensitive projects can take 9–12 months.

**The negotiation:** PD conditions are negotiated. Experienced development advisors know which conditions are standard in a given municipality, which are negotiable, and which represent overreach that should be resisted.

### TxDOT Coordination

Any DFW project with access to a state highway — a common situation in suburban development — requires coordination with the Texas Department of Transportation. TxDOT has jurisdiction over access points, drainage into state rights-of-way, and traffic impact. TxDOT review and approval can add 6–12 months to project timelines for access-sensitive sites.

## Code Advisory in Practice: Key Deliverables

Effective development code advisory is not a desktop exercise. It requires site visits, code research, staff coordination, and scenario analysis. The key deliverables:

**Pre-Acquisition Code Analysis:** Before a site is under contract, a thorough code analysis should address: current zoning and permitted uses, entitlement risk and timeline, compatibility exposure (in Austin), impervious cover constraints, height and density limits, and applicable overlay regulations.

**Program Optimization:** Given the regulatory envelope, what program maximizes project economics? Code advisory should inform design programming — not just validate it after the fact.

**Entitlement Strategy:** What approvals are needed, in what sequence, and what is the realistic timeline? Optimistic entitlement timelines are a primary source of underwriting error in Texas development.

**Risk Register:** What are the entitlement risks — and what is the cost of each risk materializing? A code advisory engagement should produce not just an optimistic scenario but a realistic range of outcomes.
